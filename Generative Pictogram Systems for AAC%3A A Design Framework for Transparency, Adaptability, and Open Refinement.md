- #[[New PGR9]] #AAC #XAI #[[Diseño de Interacción]] 
  
  **Abstract**  
  This report synthesizes current research and practice-based approaches to designing generative pictogram systems for augmentative and alternative communication (AAC). It examines architectural patterns, transparency mechanisms, and participatory frameworks that enable adaptable, locally-refinable symbol generation while maintaining semantic consistency and cognitive accessibility. The analysis centers on three core dimensions: (1) interpretable AI pipelines that expose generative decisions to stakeholders, (2) modular visual grammars based on semantic primitives that support cultural and individual adaptation, and (3) federated co-design infrastructures that distribute refinement authority across communities of practice.
- ## 1. Current State and Foundational Challenges
	- ### 1.1 The Symbol Generation Gap in AAC
		- Contemporary AAC systems face a fundamental tension between standardization and personalization. While established symbol sets (PCS, SymbolStix, ARASAAC) provide stable visual vocabularies, they lack the flexibility to accommodate cultural variation, emergent vocabulary needs, and individual cognitive profiles. Traditional symbol creation requires intensive graphic design labor, preventing "just-in-time" generation of personalized symbols. This creates a scalability bottleneck: users require symbols that match their developmental stage, linguistic background, and communicative context, but manual design processes cannot meet dynamic needs.[^1], [^2]
		- Generative artificial intelligence offers a potential solution through text-to-image and image-to-image transformation pipelines. Early pilot studies demonstrate that diffusion models (Stable Diffusion) and generative adversarial networks can produce symbols matching existing style schemas. However, three critical challenges emerge:[^3], [^1]
			- **Transparency deficit**: Black-box generation prevents users from understanding why specific visual compositions are produced, undermining trust and learnability[^4]
			- **Adaptability constraints**: Centralized models cannot incorporate local cultural modifications or individual user refinements without full retraining
			- **Refinement opacity**: Users lack mechanisms to correct generation errors or evolve symbols iteratively within their communication ecosystems
	- ### 1.2 Cognitive Accessibility Imperatives
		- Pictographic symbols operate through varying degrees of iconicity—transparent symbols where meaning is immediately apparent, translucent symbols requiring learned association, and opaque symbols demanding explicit instruction. Effective generative systems must preserve and enhance this semantic gradient while ensuring visual clarity. Research indicates that symbols depicting actions (people performing tasks) are more readily understood than static object representations, suggesting generative models should prioritize agentive compositions over isolated icons.[^5], [^1]
		- Cognitive load considerations further necessitate consistent visual grammars. Arbitrary style variations increase learning demands, particularly for users with intellectual disabilities who rely on stable symbol-referent mappings. Yet excessive rigidity prevents accommodation of individual processing preferences. The design challenge becomes: how to maintain sufficient consistency for learning while enabling meaningful personalization.[^6]
- ## 2. Architectural Approaches for Transparent Generation
	- ### 2.1 Interpretable Model Pipelines
		- Transparency in generative pictogram systems requires moving beyond post-hoc explanation toward inherently interpretable architectures. The distinction is critical: **interpretability** addresses how the model functions internally, while **explainability** justifies specific decisions after generation. For AAC stakeholders (users, families, clinicians), interpretable models align better with clinical decision-making processes that require ongoing assessment of symbol appropriateness.[^4]
		- **Hybrid Architectures** combine interpretable components with performant generative models:
			- **Generalized Additive Models (GAMs)** with attention-based neural networks enable pixel-level attribution mapping, highlighting which visual regions most influence symbol classification[^4]
			- **Neural Additive Models (NAMs)** decompose generation into semantically meaningful feature contributions, allowing stakeholders to adjust individual visual dimensions (e.g., "increase action clarity" vs. "reduce color complexity")
			- **Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)** provide more interpretable latent spaces than GANs, where symbol dimensions correspond to human-understandable visual attributes[^7]
		- Recent implementations demonstrate that classifier-free guidance (CFG) scales and denoising strength parameters can be exposed as user-controllable levers, enabling fine-grained adaptation without model retraining. This transforms opaque diffusion processes into manipulable design interfaces.[^1]
- ### 2.2 Visual Grammar as Computation
	- A generative pictogram system requires a formal visual grammar that constrains AI output to semantically valid compositions. The **PictoNet** framework proposes a meta-language based on semantic primitives—irreducible pictographic elements that combine to convey complex meanings. This approach draws from linguistic theory: just as languages build sentences from limited phonological inventories, pictogram systems can generate unlimited symbols from constrained visual primitives.[^8]
	- **Implementation Structure**:
		- | Grammar Level | Components | Example | Transparency Mechanism |
		  |---------------|------------|---------|------------------------|
		  | **Semantic Primitives** | 50-200 base concepts (EAT, GO, PERSON, GOOD)[^9] | PERSON + EAT + APPLE | Primitive selection visible in prompt |
		  | **Visual Morphology** | Style parameters (stroke width, color coding, perspective) | 3/4 view vs. profile | Parameter sliders in UI |
		  | **Syntactic Rules** | Composition constraints (agent-action-object ordering) | PERSON→EAT→APPLE | Validation feedback on ordering |
		  | **Contextual Modulation** | Cultural/ethnic appearance variants | Asian ethnicity parameter | Explicit culture selection[^10] |
	- This grammar enables **symbolic transparency**: users can inspect which primitives generated a symbol, adjust their visual manifestation, and validate semantic correctness before adoption.[^11]
- ## 3. Adaptability Frameworks: From Universal to Local
	- ### 3.1 The Universal-Local Paradox
		- AAC design confronts a paradox: symbols must be universally recognizable for broad communication, yet locally meaningful for individual identity expression. Centralized generative models resolve this poorly. Federated architectures offer a pathway: maintaining a shared base vocabulary while enabling edge adaptation.[^8]
		- **Federated Learning for Symbol Generation** distributes model training across user devices while preserving privacy. The MEC-AI HetFL framework demonstrates how edge clusters can locally adapt symbols based on:[^12]
			- Individual usage patterns (frequently selected symbols)
			- Cultural context (ethnicity, regional variations)
			- Cognitive feedback (comprehension scores, selection accuracy)
		- Rather than uploading raw interaction data, devices transmit model updates (gradients) to a central aggregator, which synthesizes improvements into the global symbol set. This enables **adaptive personalization** without compromising user privacy.
	- ### 3.2 Modular Style Transfer
		- Image-to-image style transformation provides immediate adaptability. The Global Symbols AI tool implements this through:
			- 1. **Base symbol upload**: User provides existing symbol set establishing style schema
			- 2. **Prompt engineering**: Text descriptions + style keywords ("Mulberry symbol style")[^13]
			- 3. **Parameter control**: Denoising strength (0.0-1.0) controls deviation from original style
			- 4. **Cultural modulation**: Explicit ethnicity/locale parameters adjust visual representation[^10]
		- This approach supports **progressive refinement**: symbols can evolve from closely matching existing sets toward novel compositions as user competence grows.
- ## 4. Open Refinement Pathways
	- ### 4.1 Participatory Co-Design Infrastructure
		- Effective refinement requires shifting from designer-as-expert to user-as-expert models. **Generative participatory design** methods embed stakeholders directly in the creation loop:[^14]
			- **Collage and envisioning**: Users arrange pictographic elements to express desired symbol characteristics[^15]
			- **Tangible symbol prototyping**: 3D-printed symbols allow physical manipulation and feedback before digital finalization
			- **Wizard-of-Oz testing**: Human designers simulate AI generation in real-time, capturing refinement patterns for model training
		- The **CycLink Co-design Study** protocol demonstrates adaptation for disability contexts: phased involvement options, inclusive consent processes, and interpretative support during analysis ensure meaningful participation from individuals with diverse support needs.[^16]
	- ### 4.2 Iterative Validation Loops
		- Open refinement demands continuous evaluation mechanisms. The **online voting system** piloted by Draffan et al. enables community rating of AI-generated symbols. Successful symbols (high comprehension + appropriate style match) enter the canonical set; unsuccessful ones trigger pipeline adjustments.[^1]
		- **Refinement Metrics** should include:
			- | Metric | Assessment Method | Refinement Trigger |
			  |--------|-------------------|-------------------|
			  | **Iconicity Score** | User guessability tests | If <70% correct, regenerate with clearer primitives |
			  | **Style Fidelity** | Designer expert review | If <85% match, adjust style transfer parameters |
			  | **Cultural Appropriateness** | Community stakeholder panels | If concerns raised, flag for local adaptation |
			  | **Cognitive Load** | Comprehension time measurements | If >3s average, simplify visual complexity |
	- ### 4.3 Version Control for Symbols
		- Git-based version control enables **symbol forking**: communities can branch base symbols, modify for local needs, and propose merges back to the main repository. This mirrors open-source software development, creating a **pictographic commons** where adaptations accumulate as structured contributions rather than fragmented alternatives.[^8]
- ## 5. Implementation Architectures
	- ### 5.1 Federated Generative Pipeline
		- A production-ready system requires layered architecture:
			- **Layer 1: Input Processing**
				- Text prompt parsing with semantic role labeling
				- Primitive extraction from WordNet synsets[^17]
				- Cultural context detection
			- **Layer 2: Generation Engine**
				- VAE for latent space navigation (interpretable dimensions)
				- Diffusion model for high-fidelity rendering
				- Style encoder for symbol set consistency
			- **Layer 3: Transparency Interface**
				- Attention visualization showing primitive influence
				- Parameter sliders exposing generation levers
				- Confidence scores for each visual element
			- **Layer 4: Refinement Loop**
				- User feedback collection (ratings, edits, rejections)
				- Local model fine-tuning on device
				- Federated aggregation of improvements
- ### 5.2 Edge Deployment Considerations
	- For resource-constrained AAC devices, model compression is essential. Techniques include:
		- **Knowledge distillation**: Train compact student models from larger teacher models[^7]
		- **Quantization**: Reduce precision to 8-bit integers for faster inference
		- **Pruning**: Remove redundant network parameters while maintaining symbol quality
		- **Split computing**: Execute primitive extraction locally, outsource rendering to edge servers
	- The **AMBRA framework** proposes federated learning with generative AI specifically for AAC, enabling personalised symbol generation while preserving data sovereignty.[^18]
- ## 6. Ethical and Practical Synthesis
	- ### 6.1 Addressing Symbol Proliferation
		- Unconstrained generative systems risk exacerbating symbol inconsistency problems. Mitigation strategies include:[^19]
			- **Canonical core sets**: 200-500 high-frequency symbols remain stable across users
			- **Controlled variation**: Generative parameters bound to meaningful dimensions (culture, complexity, perspective)
			- **Registration mechanism**: New symbols require community validation before widespread adoption
	- ### 6.2 Transparency as Design Material
		- Rather than treating transparency as a compliance layer, it must be integrated as a **design material**—shaped through visual grammar, interaction logic, and participatory input. This requires:[^8]
			- **Explainable defaults**: New users see simplified parameter sets; experts access full controls
			- **Progressive disclosure**: Complexity reveals based on user competence and interest
			- **Audit trails**: Every symbol carries metadata about its generation parameters, training data, and refinement history
	- ### 6.3 Inclusive Governance
		- Open refinement demands governance structures that center disabled users. The Microsoft Visual Studio IntelliCode case demonstrates that disability-led co-design spurs innovation by revealing latent needs invisible to non-disabled developers. Governance should include:[^14]
			- **Disability-majority steering committees**
			- **Paid co-researcher positions** for AAC users
			- **Accessible contribution pathways** accommodating various communication modes
- ## 7. Recommendations for Implementation
- ### 7.1 Phased Development Roadmap
	- **Phase 1: Foundation (6 months)**
		- Establish semantic primitive set through participatory workshops
		- Develop interpretable VAE architecture with attention visualization
		- Create base style encoders for 3-5 major symbol sets (ARASAAC, Mulberry, PCS)
	- **Phase 2: Pilot (12 months)**
		- Deploy federated learning infrastructure across 50-100 AAC users
		- Implement co-design tooling for symbol refinement
		- Conduct longitudinal comprehension studies measuring iconicity retention
	- **Phase 3: Scale (18 months)**
		- Open-source core architecture under permissive license
		- Establish symbol commons governance model
		- Integrate with existing AAC platforms via API
- ### 7.2 Technical Standards
	- **Format**: SVG with semantic markup tagging primitives (e.g., `<g class="primitive-agent">`)
	- **Metadata**: JSON-LD embedding generation parameters, style lineage, and cultural context
	- **API**: RESTful interface exposing transparency endpoints (`GET /symbol/{id}/attribution`)
	- **Privacy**: Differential privacy in federated aggregation, local differential privacy on device
- ### 7.3 Evaluation Framework
	- Success metrics must balance technical performance with human outcomes:
	- **System metrics**: Symbol generation latency (<2s), style fidelity (>90%), parameter interpretability
	- **User metrics**: Comprehension accuracy (>80%), selection speed improvement (>15% over baseline), user satisfaction (NASA-TLX cognitive load reduction)
	- **Community metrics**: Symbol contribution rate, fork-to-merge ratio, cross-cultural adoption
- ## Conclusion
	- Designing generative pictogram systems that are transparent, adaptable, and open to refinement requires reimagining AI not as a black-box backend but as a **participatory design material**. The synthesis of interpretable architectures, federated learning, visual grammars based on semantic primitives, and disability-led co-design creates a pathway toward symbol systems that honor both universal accessibility and individual identity.
	- The research demonstrates that technical transparency must be coupled with social transparency—clear governance, inclusive participation, and recognition of users as experts of their own experience. As generative AI capabilities expand, the AAC community has an opportunity to establish precedents for ethical, participatory AI development that centers the needs of historically marginalized communicators.
	- Future work should prioritize longitudinal studies examining how iteratively refined symbols affect language development over time, and how federated architectures can balance local adaptation with global coherence. The goal is not perfect automation, but **augmented design intelligence**—systems that amplify human creativity and expertise while respecting the autonomy and agency of AAC users.

				- Text prompt parsing with semantic role labeling
				- Primitive extraction from WordNet synsets[^17]
				- Cultural context detection
			- **Layer 2: Generation Engine**
				- VAE for latent space navigation (interpretable dimensions)
				- Diffusion model for high-fidelity rendering
				- Style encoder for symbol set consistency
			- **Layer 3: Transparency Interface**
				- Attention visualization showing primitive influence
				- Parameter sliders exposing generation levers
				- Confidence scores for each visual element
			- **Layer 4: Refinement Loop**
				- User feedback collection (ratings, edits, rejections)
				- Local model fine-tuning on device
				- Federated aggregation of improvements
- ### 5.2 Edge Deployment Considerations
	- For resource-constrained AAC devices, model compression is essential. Techniques include:
		- **Knowledge distillation**: Train compact student models from larger teacher models[^7]
		- **Quantization**: Reduce precision to 8-bit integers for faster inference
		- **Pruning**: Remove redundant network parameters while maintaining symbol quality
		- **Split computing**: Execute primitive extraction locally, outsource rendering to edge servers
	- The **AMBRA framework** proposes federated learning with generative AI specifically for AAC, enabling personalised symbol generation while preserving data sovereignty.[^18]
- ## 6. Ethical and Practical Synthesis
	- ### 6.1 Addressing Symbol Proliferation
		- Unconstrained generative systems risk exacerbating symbol inconsistency problems. Mitigation strategies include:[^19]
			- **Canonical core sets**: 200-500 high-frequency symbols remain stable across users
			- **Controlled variation**: Generative parameters bound to meaningful dimensions (culture, complexity, perspective)
			- **Registration mechanism**: New symbols require community validation before widespread adoption
	- ### 6.2 Transparency as Design Material
		- Rather than treating transparency as a compliance layer, it must be integrated as a **design material**—shaped through visual grammar, interaction logic, and participatory input. This requires:[^8]
			- **Explainable defaults**: New users see simplified parameter sets; experts access full controls
			- **Progressive disclosure**: Complexity reveals based on user competence and interest
			- **Audit trails**: Every symbol carries metadata about its generation parameters, training data, and refinement history
	- ### 6.3 Inclusive Governance
		- Open refinement demands governance structures that center disabled users. The Microsoft Visual Studio IntelliCode case demonstrates that disability-led co-design spurs innovation by revealing latent needs invisible to non-disabled developers. Governance should include:[^14]
			- **Disability-majority steering committees**
			- **Paid co-researcher positions** for AAC users
			- **Accessible contribution pathways** accommodating various communication modes
- ## 7. Recommendations for Implementation
- ### 7.1 Phased Development Roadmap
	- **Phase 1: Foundation (6 months)**
		- Establish semantic primitive set through participatory workshops
		- Develop interpretable VAE architecture with attention visualization
		- Create base style encoders for 3-5 major symbol sets (ARASAAC, Mulberry, PCS)
	- **Phase 2: Pilot (12 months)**
		- Deploy federated learning infrastructure across 50-100 AAC users
		- Implement co-design tooling for symbol refinement
		- Conduct longitudinal comprehension studies measuring iconicity retention
	- **Phase 3: Scale (18 months)**
		- Open-source core architecture under permissive license
		- Establish symbol commons governance model
		- Integrate with existing AAC platforms via API
- ### 7.2 Technical Standards
	- **Format**: SVG with semantic markup tagging primitives (e.g., `<g class="primitive-agent">`)
	- **Metadata**: JSON-LD embedding generation parameters, style lineage, and cultural context
	- **API**: RESTful interface exposing transparency endpoints (`GET /symbol/{id}/attribution`)
	- **Privacy**: Differential privacy in federated aggregation, local differential privacy on device
- ### 7.3 Evaluation Framework
	- Success metrics must balance technical performance with human outcomes:
	- **System metrics**: Symbol generation latency (<2s), style fidelity (>90%), parameter interpretability
	- **User metrics**: Comprehension accuracy (>80%), selection speed improvement (>15% over baseline), user satisfaction (NASA-TLX cognitive load reduction)
	- **Community metrics**: Symbol contribution rate, fork-to-merge ratio, cross-cultural adoption
- ## Conclusion
	- Designing generative pictogram systems that are transparent, adaptable, and open to refinement requires reimagining AI not as a black-box backend but as a **participatory design material**. The synthesis of interpretable architectures, federated learning, visual grammars based on semantic primitives, and disability-led co-design creates a pathway toward symbol systems that honor both universal accessibility and individual identity.
	- The research demonstrates that technical transparency must be coupled with social transparency—clear governance, inclusive participation, and recognition of users as experts of their own experience. As generative AI capabilities expand, the AAC community has an opportunity to establish precedents for ethical, participatory AI development that centers the needs of historically marginalized communicators.
	- Future work should prioritize longitudinal studies examining how iteratively refined symbols affect language development over time, and how federated architectures can balance local adaptation with global coherence. The goal is not perfect automation, but **augmented design intelligence**—systems that amplify human creativity and expertise while respecting the autonomy and agency of AAC users.
